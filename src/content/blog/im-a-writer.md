### I Want to Tell Stories. I Just Don’t Like Writing Them.

I‘m a writer. Like many other writers, I tend to immediately hate almost everything I write.  
I can imagine places, histories and strange little quirks about their worlds quite happily. I can picture abandoned buildings in desolate villages and work out who use lived there, based on the ruins they’ve left behind. I can invent mask and cloak wearing organisations, with absurd rituals and technologies, and the disasters they shepherd. I can spend far too long thinking about what might be written on a faded note pinned to a wall in a room that doesn’t exist.  
The second I try to turn any of that into prose, something just changes.  
Ideas become too stiff. Characters feel artificial. Sentences become things to analyse, not enjoy. I reread paragraphs so many times it stops sounding like language. Words become nonsense abstracts.  
Then, usually, I decide it’s terrible and pointless and derivative and shitty and I just abandon it. It’s now happened enough that I‘ve started to think I’m trying to tell these stories ‘the wrong way’, or in the wrong medium.  



---

### Stories exist before the writing does

I don’t lack ideas. The stories are there, though they’re rarely in a neat beginning-to-end form. They’re fragmented, incomplete and hanging in a setting with no firm ground:

- locations with history
- events that have already happened
- two people remembering it differently
- a McGuffin, whose importance isn’t clear
- systems that continued operating long after anyone understood their rationale
- Documents which contradict what the reader already knows...

I tend to think about stories spatially rather than sequentially, maybe because of my wonky brain-shape…?  
Instead of narration leading the reader through a plot, I prefer your ignorant arrival, before gradually work out what has happened here. The story is told across rooms, objects, records, conversations, but also absence. Meaning comes from the relationships between those things; meaning is constructed by the reader, not fed to them like grey porridge.  
That may be why I struggle in writing a ‘traditional story’, and it often feels uncomfortable. I am trying to flatten the experience of place, person and context into a 1D sequence of paragraphs.  
I don’t want to tell the reader where to look, as much as I don’t think I need to be told… I want to build a ‘somewhere’ the reader is compelled to explore.



---

### A story the reader uncovers

I decided a little while back that a long-term ambition is to manifest some of these unfinished stories through an exploration game. To give them a life that the page can’t realise.  
Not necessarily a game around combat, or levelling, or a stream of fetch-objectives. I’m interested in creating world that reward your attention and pay dividends to the curious explorer.  
The reader moves through an environment, finds ‘evidence’, notices connections and gradually forms an understanding of events which isn’t necessarily ‘shared’. Sometimes details are explicit, while others will only make sense when examined alongside some other discovery, much later.  
The stories don’t exist isolated within dialogue or cutscenes… never a cutscene. They live woven into the environment. A locked room might reveal who desired secrecy, or authority. The cluttered workspace might reveal why someone left, or when they’ll return. A secret message might make an unsympathetic character seem human. An empty space may not matter as much with an object placed within it.  
Readers aren’t just open conduits to mindlessly ‘receive’ the story. They should need to participate in reconstructing it, or not, if they’d prefer!



---

### Avoiding writing by choosing an even harder form of writing

The obvious flaw with this plan is that even narrative within games *still require writing*.  
They require dialogue, descriptions, notes, environmental text, character histories, item names and countless other small pieces of language. They require design, programming, art, audio, testing and a level of coordination that makes writing a short story appear comparatively sensible.  
Creating an exploration game because I find prose difficult may be the creative equivalent of avoiding a walk by deciding to invent teleportation. But the difference, for me at least, is that my hateful writing no longer needs to carry the entire experience by itself. Of course, this doesn’t remove the need for me to write well, it just changes the job the writing needs to do.



---

### Letting fragments remain fragments

One of the things I find liberating about environmental storytelling is that fragments don’t necessarily need to be expanded into explanations; half-written letters can remain unfinished, or recordings can end too soon, or spoken records can be contradictory.  
The character is allowed to misunderstand what’s happened; *the reader can be left with uncertainty*.  
Traditional writing does all of these things too, of course, but an explorable environment makes them natural. Missing information is another part of the world, rather than glaring evidence that I failed to finish a chapter. And that suits the way ideas take hold in my consciousness.  
I don’t always know a full story immediately. Sometimes I only get a feel for a place that might have existed, and that something happened there, and that someone has gone to considerable effort to conceal it, or to stop them. Building from those fragments feels way more achievable than forcing *another* chapter opening paragraph.



---

### The danger of the permanent long-term project

Calling this a long-term goal is both honest and dangerous. ‘Long-term’ means ambitious, and patient. It also means distant: ideas which remain perfect, because they’re never started.  
It’s far too easy to spend years thinking about the game *I might make*. I can design histories, sketch maps, compare game engines, plan mechanics, and create endless documents, without ever actually producing something another person could play.  
So, my challenge is not deciding what this game should be but is in deciding what the smallest version of the idea might look like. We’ll start at one location, maybe a few rooms; one ‘event’ and a handful of objects.  
A story that can be understood differently depending on what the reader finds.  
No sprawling world. No elaborate mechanics. No decade-long fictional history that must be fully documented before the first door can be opened.  
Just a small place with something to discover.



---

### Learning to tolerate unfinished work

To create this, I need to learn to become comfortable with work that I don’t think is complete, or is awkward or is just not very good.  
Disliking my own writing is my biggest excuse to stop writing. When the first version does not sound like the finished version, it’s evidence that my ideas have failed.  
I’ve an idea that game development won’t permit me the convenience of that illusion. Environments will be empty, until they aren’t. Interactions will feel clumsy, until they’re reconciled against the environment. The first dialogue will be unnatural, until it fits a wider narrative. The first build may barely qualify as a game, but It’ll represent a place that’s growing. 'Perfect' is well renowned as the enemy of 'good'.  
I can understand this pretty well when working on technical projects; prototypes exist to answer questions and early versions expose assumptions. Iteration is expected. I just have trouble in applying that thinking to a creative work.



---

### What I *actually want* to create

I don’t think my goal is to ‘make a game’. I want to create the feeling of entering a place that existed before the reader arrived. Places shaped by people who had their own priorities, weaknesses and private lives. Places where events have left marks. Places that don’t gift wrap and explanation.  
I want the reader to notice the small things and wonder if they matter. I want them to theory-craft, revisioning explanations occasionally when they realise they’ve maybe interpreted something which doesn’t fit. I want stories to feel discovered, not delivered.  
But, most of all, I want to find a form that allows these stories in my head to exist *somewhere else,* please.



---

### Starting before I'm ready

I’m still a writer; a crap one, ‘aspiring’, as we all are to express meaning through language. I dislike *pretty much everything I produce*, including the sentences that seemed perfectly acceptable five minutes before I reread them. I’ve not solved that problem, nor do I think I ever shall.  
But maybe my aim isn’t to reach a point where I approve of every written word. Perhaps it’s to instead stop requiring that level of scrutinous approval before I allow my ideas to exist. The exploration game remains my long-term ambition, in lieu of a rich and compelling written universe. It requires a bunch of skills I don’t even have, commitment to experiments that’ll crap out and a great deal of actual ‘proper’ writing that I’ll invariably rewrite, rephrase and then delete.

At the moment, though, I’ve some new first objectives, and they’re smaller:

*Create a place; put a story inside it; let someone find it*.
